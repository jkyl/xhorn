August 1, 2016 - Cold load calibration

Christopher D. Sheehy

Intro

We have been seeing wiggles in the ratio plots of P(am). See here. It seemed like the wiggles had the same period as the standing waves generated by the long cable connecting the IF amp output to the ROACH box. This seemed strange because the waves should cancel in the ratio if they are stable. Increasing the attenuation just before the cable / ROACH box interface eliminated the standing waves, and it seemed like the ratio wiggles decreased as well, but they did not go away. Compare Figs 5 and 6 here, and especially Figs 4-6 here, where the decrease in ratio structure is much reduced over a long scanning period.

The ratio structure is better, but still R is not flat. We therefore took the electronics and antennas down to the lab to investigate.

The last I could not measure because even with a 20 dB attenuator on the power meter input, this power overloads it. I am simply adding the measured broadband IF amp gain. This is too much power. The ZFL2000 IF amp has a +16 dBm 1dB compression point. Steve says that is for CV, and for noise we want 10 dBm lower, so +6. Also, in this configuration, I could not detect any decrease in power when dunking the terminator in LN2.

I therefore moved the 2 GHz lowpass filter from just before the ROACH to just after the mixer. I noticed high S11 and S22 parameters for the mixer, as well, so I added -6 dB attenuation just after it. (This proved too much and I took some out later.)

The change in the LNA amplified power due to a delta T of (290-77) K is 4.2e-5 mW. So 290 K gives ~5e-5 mW = -43 dBm of power at the output of the LNA. If the LNA gain is 40 dB, 290 K is -83 dBm of in band power at the input of the LNA. The amplifier itself contributes another 60 K = -88 dBm at the input. Because this is noise power, if hook up the VNA to the LNA input, we need to show linearity roughly between -100 and -75 dBm input power.

The VNA says it will output arbitrarily small output powers, but measuring it, it actually has a minimum at -43 dBm. So I put 60 dB attenuation on the LNA input and swept the VNA between -40 dBm and 0 dBm at 10, 10.5, 11 and 11.5 GHz (in scalar mixer mode, LO=9.5 GHz.) All S parameters appeared fully linear up to 0 dBm.

Later, measurements of Tsys showed nosie temperatures ~200 K. I discovered this was due to low gain. I removed the 6 dB attenuator after the attenuator and reinstated AMP2 just before the mixer. The noise temperatures went down to 60-100 K, and this is the configuration in all the plots below. In this configuration, non-linearity was apparent at 0 dBm (really -60 dBm) input power, but not -15 (-75) dBm, which is the spec we wanted to meet.

I will post the S parameters in the final configuration in another posting.

Terminator

I first attached the terminator to the LNA input and dunked it in LN2, watching it cool down. Using the mean of the first 6 and last 6 spectra to calibrate the intermediate spectra. Figure 1a shows the raw spectra, a single frequency channel as a function of time, and the calibrated spectra. The first and last few spectra will be flat by definition, but if the terminator actually has reflections, the intermediate spectra will (I think) not calibrate out to be flat. This is like calibrating to a non-flat source and injecting wiggles into an actually flat sky spectrum. Figure 1b shows a second run doing the exact same thing, to demonstrate repeatability.

Pyramidal horn looking at metal box eccosorb load

I then replace the terminator with the pyramidal horn staring into the metal box eccosorb load. The metal box load does not have eccosorb baffling on the inner walls, so I am relying on the cones being beam filling. Figure 5 shows a picture.

Fig. 5 - Calibration setup
 

I then spray the cones with LN2, put it in the beam, and watch it warm up. I do not know T_cold, nor do I know the intermediate temperatures. However, an incorrect assumption for the load temperature results in a flat absolute calibration error but will not inject frequency dependent structure. The load is not isothermal at all, but we are in the RJ, and the summation of blackbodies of many different temperatures is the summation of many flat lines of different slope, which makes another flat line. Therefore, non-isothermalness ought to not inject structure either.

The following plots are the same as the terminator plots, except we have warming up data, so the ratio plot uses a different spectrum in the denominator. I have assumed Tcold = 190, which gets the Tsys in about the right ballpark. However, this is considerably uncertain, and I would guess that the total system temperature increases quite a bit compared to a terminated LNA input.

Immediately obvious are the huge standing waves in Figure 8. The fundamental frequency is ~20 MHz, which corresponds to 15 m. The standing waves line up perfectly in the frequency shifted spectra, meaning they were in the RF. This looks like a reflection into the room somewhere, at about the distance to the back wall or ceiling. With the load warm, I noticed that moving it by hand changes the raw spectrum noticeably. So either the source is reflecting or there are some strong side/backlobes seeing the walls. To test this, I put a piece of AN-73 behind the horn and did a with/without comparison of a few raw spectra, and they were significantly different. This indicated a strong backlobe to me. This is maybe not too surprising since the pyramidal horn has not great beam characteristics. They are not terrible, probably, but a 1 K standing wave from the horn putting out 60 K of LNA noise temperature is around -20 dB, so it might not need to be awful to explain what we see. Nonetheless, these reflections and standing waves can't be stable in time, otherwise they would cancel in the ratio plot. So I'm not sure why this would be. Could we be seeing radiation emitted from the source, bouncing off a wall, and coming back to the horn? This reflection would be function of load temperature and would cause structure in the ratio.

Corrugated horn looking at metal box eccosorb load

I then replaced the pyramidal horn with the corrugated horn + baffle. This will hugely suppress sidelobes and backlobes. I did two runs of this. Figure 10 shows a picture.

Looking at Fig 13, runs 1 and 2 look quite different. There are still standing waves, but they look large scale and not in the RF in run 1, and much more coherent and in the RF in run 2. However, the ratio plot shows clear features that align in the RF but not in the IF. So I don't know what to make of it. I was thinking that ice could have accumulated during run 1 and reflected during run 2, but this does not happen in the next section, so I think that hypothesis is out. The standing waves in run 2 are ~60 MHz. This is a 5 m length, so seems to exclude the source itself. It could be more complicated, with the source reflecting into the room and this coming back into the atenna. Still, the 1 K features seem to imply a level of sidelobe pickup that I know we don't have with the horn (see this posting). We can't measure beyond a boresight angle of 60 deg, but sidelobes appear to be < -60 dB.

Is the difference between runs 1 and 2 a slightly different position of the box?

I then replaced the metal box with the wood eccosorb box. This box is deeper and has eccosorb baffling lining the inner walls. If there are reflectios off these walls, these will be suppressed. If there are direct lines of sight into the room, these will be suppressed as well since the box is deeper. Other than that, nothing is different.

Runs 1 and 2 look very consistent. All the calibrated spectra have significantly reduced features. It seems the largest wavelength features from Fig 13a (and certainly Fig 13b) are suppressed. These features (which are maybe only features in retrospect) look like they have a fundamental of ~ 500 MHz, which corresponds to 60 cm. This could possible be a reflection within the calibrator. There are definitely still features in the RF corresponding to ~5 m distances, which appear to be off some walls. So I think we might be seeing something in the room somewhere which is not stable in time.

Conclusions

With the baffled, corruated horn looking at the calibrator, we are definitely seeing something other than at -50 dB blackbody. It is originiating somewhere in front of the LNA input because we have demonstrated impressive flatness with a terminated load. We need to reproduce this flatness with the horn looking at a calibrator. So far we have not done this. Not only are the calibrated intermediate temperature spectra not flat, but the ratio of differences of raw spectra is not flat, indicating that whatever non-flat signal we are seeing is not stable in time.

We will investiate taking the comoving baffle off and adding more eccosorb baffling to the eccosorb load.
