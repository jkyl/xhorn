August 19, 2016 - Cone calibrator reflection measurement

Christopher D. Sheehy

Intro

Here are measurements of the reflections from the VHP eccosorb cone calibrator. We constructed an AN-73 lined lid for the box and made measurements with and without it.

Method

Figure 1 shows the setup.

With the baffled corrugated horn staring at the calibrator, we hook up the corrugated horn to the signal generator and transmit at 10 GHz. We receive with a relatively high gain pyramidal horn and read out through the same amplifier / mixer / ROACH chain. We chop the transmit signal and plot the received on/off differece spectrum. This gives us awesome s/n in real time on any signal coming out of the box.

We turn the signal generator down to -80 dBm and put the pyramidal horn right in the aperture of the corrugated horn. We note the received differential power in ADU^2>. (We checked that swapping out the pyramidal horn for a bare WR-10 waveguide that the decrease in ADU^2 is roughly consistent with the decrease in aperture area.) We make a small correction to this power by multiplying by 1.3, the ratio of aperture areas of the horns, Acorrug/Apyramidal. We take this to be the total power available to be absorbed/reflected, which we denote Ptot.

Then we put the eccosorb box back in place and crank the signal generator up to 0 dBm. (We checked that with 60 dB of attenuation in front of the amplifiers, cranking up to 0 dBm results in the expected 10^6 increase in ADU^2.) We then measure in 13 different locations around the box at a distance of 18'' from a fixed point centered on the horn. The reflected radiation is in mostly in the same polarization as the broadcast radiation. We spot checked that turning the pyramidal horn by 90 deg reduced the received power by >10x.

Results

Figure 2 shows Pmeas / (Ptot*1e8), interpolated onto a regular lat/lon grid (with wrapping enforced in longitude), and expressed in dB. The markers show locations where measurements were made.

Fig. 1 - Received reflected power
 

To obtain the total reflectivity, we divide the interpolated Pmeas / (Ptot*1e8) by the aperture area of the pyramidal horn (18.125 in^2) and integrate over the sphere (radius 18''). This is equivalent to integrating the received power in ADU^2 / in^2 over the area of the sphere and dividing by the total transmitted power in ADU^2. The total reflectivity is shown in the text annotation in Figure 1.

All the reflections show a decrease when adding the absorber lined lid except for the location closest to directly behind the horn (actually at lat=-20). We interpret this as a backlobe at approx. -67 dB. We therefore try excluding this point from the integration, instead interpolating over it. This should best estimate the reflectivity of the calibrator box itself. Including this point estimates the total response of the horn to power originating outside the box. One thing that is not measured is a direct reflection from the corrugated horn, off the cones, and directly back into the horn. Given the geometry, it seems unlikely that this reflection would be favored over others. I tried putting the horn on the VNA to measure S11, but there is no time domain option and I could not separate out the reflections off the cones from reflections at shorter delays. We instead reversed the experimental setup here and broadcast with the pyramidal horn and received with the corrugated horn. We stuck the pyramidal horn almost inside the box, as close to the horn as we could, and shined it all around inside the box, pointed at the cones. We never saw any received power greater than -60 dB.

Conclusions

The calibrator with a baffled horn and no lid is very black, with reflections = -57 dB. The VHP-12 eccosorb cones have an expected reflectivity of ~-65 dB at 10 GHz (see here). Adding the absorber lined lid reduces the power making it out of the box to -63 dB. There is probably a backlobe with total integrated power of around -65 dB as well.
